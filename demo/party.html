A crowd steers one shared party through the attested dungeon — by vote. Each turn: open a vote over the party's candidate moves, the seated adventurers cast their ballots, close it, and the winning move is resolved by the same engine as single-player. The crowd decides; the world still resolves — a party that votes to force a locked door is still refused, and must vote again.

What is actually real here

  • The crowd decides the party's next command on the real collective-choice engine — the quorum-certified voting substrate The Commons governs on: each ballot is a WriteOnce cap-bounded turn on a factory-born ballot cell, the tally is Monotonic, and a round certifies only once the polis AffineLe quorum gate (M = 3 of the 5-seat roster) admits the decision-turn. A quorum-met close emits a verifiable quorum certificate, not a bare count — and an independent light-client replay of the cast log recomputes the same board.
  • The world resolves the certified winner through the same /game/act path as single-player: the narrator narrates, resolve_action decides, a legal move lands as one verified turn on the receipt rail. Collective choice does not bypass the gates.
  • A party that votes a locked exit gets it resolved and refused by the world — no receipt (the anti-ghost tooth), the room unchanged — and must open another vote. Choosing the wrong move is a crowd's right; walking through a barred door is not a crowd's power.
  • The truth is the ledger, not the vote. Every landed turn is authentic (fixture leg), well-formed, injection-free, and prev-linked on-chain — a stranger can replay the whole run.

Honest scope: the quorum-certified tally is over demo identities — each seat's electorate key is blake3(name). A production deployment binds each seat to a real custody key and a signed ballot; that custody binding is the one labeled gap. The quorum mechanism, the WriteOnce ballots, the Monotonic tally, and the light-client recomputation are all real verified turns. The narration is a real local model (gemma2:2b) when reachable, else a deterministic scripted narrator (shown above).
